Overview

RoadSync is a logistics payment software with a variety of users, both B2B and B2C. Its B2B users consist of Warehouses, Carrier Services, and Tow & Repair while its B2C users are the truck drivers making deliveries. RoadSync’s goal is to expand its B2C user base and establish itself as the go-to application for these drivers on the road.

As the first in-house designer at RoadSync, I've been the sole designer for over a year. During this time, I've had the opportunity to work in all areas of design — product, UX, visual, and graphic — while discovering ways to activate and delight this interesting user base.

Branded Illustrations

Although my main role was in UX, I also had a lot of fun creating branded illustrations and design assets in Adobe Illustrator to give a little more personality to RoadSync's otherwise no-frills app.

Research

Because RoadSync is such a young company, we needed to do a lot of research about our users. Before we could start product discovery, we needed to figure out what truck drivers wanted and needed out of their digital experiences. To develop that empathy, we needed to learn more about them as people. Unfortunately, we ran into one big problem: it was difficult to find drivers interested in providing feedback for us.

So, we were left pondering the question: "How do you get to know users that don't want to be known?"


Preliminary Exploration via Social Media

For collecting feedback about specific features.

Where it helps

Typeform is helpful for getting quick opinions from users on specific offerings or features. It's also a good way to quantifiably measure user satisfaction via rating system.

Where it's not helpful

Feedback wasn't very detailed. Sometimes it was just a 1-star rating accompanied by an expletive with no further explanation.

We could ask for feedback about a specific feature, but this wasn't very helpful in terms of generalized feedback. We couldn't use this method to get a more personal or intimate reading of our users as human beings.

For when you just want to "talk to a human".

In today's age of paid listicles and SEO, you need to append "reddit" to every Google search to avoid the work of some poor writer-turned-shill touting whatever product they need to keep from starving.

Where it helps

We found that users of the r/truckers subreddit are incredibly knowledgeable and willing to share detailed information about the trucking industry.

Reddit comments proved to be contain a lot of valuable info, even (perhaps, especially) the ones dripping with sarcasm and general distaste for humanity.

Where it's not helpful

Reddit's user base is a bit younger and more technically-savvy than the average truck driver. The perspective here can be a little limited, especially when it comes to insight about accessibility.

Affinity Mapping

As we learned more about our users, we realized we had more information than we knew what to do with. To synthesize this data so it could be used in a meaningful way, we created Affinity Maps in Mural and Miro. These Affinity Maps were a nice framework to build personas off of.

RoadSync Invoices

A digital invoicing system enabling truckers to invoice their broker or carrier from their mobile device.

Lumper Fees

Drivers pay $50-$400 fee called a lumper fee that comes out of their own pocket.

Once a truck driver arrives at their destination, someone comes out to the dock to unload the contents of the truck. This saves the driver the time and energy it takes to unload their truck themselves, at a price.

Slow Payments

Drivers may not get paid for a delivery for up to 2-4 months.

Carriers and brokers don't process payments or reimbursements for drivers until they receive a proof of delivery and invoice from them.
