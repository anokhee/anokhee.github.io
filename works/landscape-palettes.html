Landscapes & Palettes

Generating mountainous landscapes & color palettes with code.

Using techniques from computational geometry, I developed a 2D vector landscapes generator. The code was written with p5.js, along with some HTML and CSS interfacing. The functionality is purposely minimal. I focused on the variety and visual appeal of the output versus on the flashiness of the application itself.

Appalachian romanticism.

In parts of Georgia bordering North Carolina, the Blue Ridge Mountains hug the horizon from every conceivable angle. While driving through small towns dotted by pastures, warehouses, and gas stations, the cozy adjacency to the mountains always makes me feel something. I wanted to express this outside of photography somehow. As a programmer, I thought that an interesting way to go about it would be with code.

Process

Minimal, but impactful.

During sketching, I aimed to keep the scope of this project as small as possible. I wanted to focus mostly on generating output interesting and unique enough to keep users entertained for a little while.

Mountains: Layers of 1D Perlin Noise

By applying a Perlin noise function with varying levels of noise detail to vector coordinates across the x and y axes, I was able to achieve output resembling a mountain range fading into the distance.

p5.js's built-in noise() function is powerful, but requires significant tweaking to achieve a desired look. I determined the "sweet spot" to base the mountains off to exist somewhere between images 4 and 7 (or 6 and 8 for mobile users).

Concluding Notes

A program like this could have reduced the hours the hours I spent drawing landscapes with Adobe Illustrator's pen tool. It made me think:

  • What is the inherent value of an organic, procedurally-generated shape compared to an identical one drawn by hand?
  • How do you compare the complexity of a program capable of generating an infinite number of unique graphics to the talent of a graphic designer?
  • How special is uniqueness, really, when it's produced with an algorithm?

I don't know!
