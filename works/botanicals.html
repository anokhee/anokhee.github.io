Spring of 2020

Unbeknownst to most people, the gloomy winter of late 2019 would precede the coronavirus pandemic and a prolonged period of anxiety and loneliness for many people around the world. So, when spring 2020 arrived in Atlanta, the bright foliage and accompanying blooms felt especially interesting and beautiful.

During walks around my neighborhood, I collected leaves and flowers to press, dry, and hang up on the walls in my apartment. Around this time, I was also religiously watching The Coding Trainâ€™s p5.js tutorials on Youtube. Process

01. Basic Fractals

Basic fractal trees look plantlike, but they resemble tree structures more than they resemble flowers. While I liked the results of this method, I wanted to explore a more natural and flowery-looking approach.


02. Fancypants Grammar for Fancy Plants

L-Systems are a type of computational grammar used to generate complex, self-similar fractals. They can model natural phenomena, such as plant growth and animal morphology.

Note on L-Systems

An L-System begins with a character string (called an axiom). Until the defined exit condition is met, the ruleset is continuously, recursively applied to the original axiom. The result is a complex character string serving as an instruction set for the flower's stem:

Concluding thoughts

  1. If I could export the output of this program to SVG, how much would it reduce the hours the hours I spend drawing landscapes with Adobe Illustrator's pen tool?
  2. What is the inherent value of an organic, procedurally-generated shape compared to an identical one drawn by hand?
  3. How do you compare the complexity of a program capable of generating an infinite number of unique graphics to the talent of a graphic designer?
  4. How special is uniqueness, really, when it's produced with an algorithm?
