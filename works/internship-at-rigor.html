Analytics Redesign at Rigor

About Rigor

Rigor, now acquired by Splunk, was once a SaaS solution providing users with powerful tools to optimize and track their website's performance. Users could access metrics related to user experience, APIs, and uptime or benchmark their site's performance against industry leaders. With this data, they could diagnose and repair technical issues or answer business questions.

Redesigning an existing feature

During the concluding week of my internship, I was given an opportunity to pursue my design interests via a 2-week solo project. The instruction was to find an area of Rigor's app that could be improved and design/develop a functional prototype to support it.

At the end of the two weeks, I presented the idea to the engineering team. The goal wasn't to deliver a production-ready new feature, but to spark discussion about user needs while applying modern web design paradigms to the existing app.

Problem Space

Reimagining website analytics reports

Research & Design


Identifying pain points

My first step was to identify the major pain points of the current implementation. Due to the time constraint, I wasn't able to conduct user interviews to understand the customers' mindsets. Luckily, I was able to rely on my coworkers and information gleaned from Rigor's product board for insight about user frustrations. Knowing that I would program the prototype into Rigor's existing React application, I broke the current chart down into its most basic components.

Overview of pain points


  1. Horizontal scrolling when users select too many metrics
  2. Poor information hierarchy makes it difficult for users to derive meaningful information from the page at a glance
  3. Unnecessary UI components like tooltips, flourishes, links, and misleading iconography clutter the page and distract from the “story” the data should be telling about the performance metrics
  4. Crowded layout with small text may be inaccessible to users with poor vision

Understanding users

Using information gathered from cross-departmental meetings and product boards, I created two personas. These personas are reflective of Rigor's primary user base with emphasis on technical experience, and personality traits.

Charts that answer business questions

My favorite part was figuring out how charts can answer business questions. Often, charts exist only for the sake of interactivity and visualization - I wanted to avoid that here. Bar charts are ubiquitous and all this data could be plotted along an X and Y axis easily, so I opted for them during this project.

The toxic relationship of D3 & React

D3 and React manipulate the DOM differently. This meant I had to brute-force some arguably disgusting code to build the interactivity and styling of the charts. With more time, I would have researched alternative libraries or methods of integrating the two libraries.

Normalizing data

At the beginning, I had some difficulty visualizing the data in a way that was meaningful at a glance. The data is all displayed in wildly incompatible units like bytes, seconds, and counts, so what might be the ideal file count would almost certainly be an impossible byte size, and would not be helpful if displayed on the same graph.

After speaking with my supervisor, he suggested that the data might be more meaningful to users if it was converted into percentiles based on Amazon Web Services' Alexa Top Sites data.To break down the percentile ranks from the 0th - 99th, we configured AWS to generate a JSON file with discrete data.

Now that I had the data in percentile ranks, I quickly encountered another issue: I would have to convert up to 3,860 unique values into individual percentiles. To accomplish this, I wrote an algorithm that normalized those values and mapped them to a D3 chart.

Concluding thoughts


Working on a large project within a limited timeframe was interesting. I learned the importance of careful planning, research, and teamwork in software development. With more time I would:

  1. Experiment with ways to effectively display data visualizations on mobile devices
  2. Conduct user interviews and tests to gain extra insight into pain points for future iterations
  3. Beautify the lefthand information panel with graphics and animations to provide feedback and bring joy to users
  4. Research additional methods of effective storytelling with data

When designing analytics reports and visualizations, I learned that it’s important to remember who the audience is. For example, a simple solution might work best for a less technical user, but devs like having the option to configure things. I also learned that even the most experienced users of your application don’t have as clear an understanding of how the data is as you do as an engineer. Bar charts, pie charts, and line graphs are well-known forms of data visualization, but extra care must taken to ensure that they don't exist only as a visual component, but to tell meaningful stories about the data.
