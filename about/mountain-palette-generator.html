Mountain Palettes Generator



Inspiration & Background

Appalachian romanticism.

In northern Georgia, bordering the Carolinas, the Blue Ridge mountains hug the horizon from almost every angle. In sparsely-populated small towns dotted by pastures, warehouses, and gas stations, the snug adjacency to these mountains can evoke a certain emotional affect. Depending on the time of day, weather, viewing angle, or even personal mood, the landscapes sometimes seem to shapeshift accordingly. Whether that leads to daydreams of an agrarian life in pre-colonial America or scenes from Deliverance is variable, but interesting.


I felt inclined to somehow artistically express this phenomenon. I've always loved subtle patterns in nature like those expressed by the unfurling of plants or drawn onto the delicate wings of moths. I figured that an interesting way to go about this project would be to simulate it with the quintessential patternmaker of today: code.

With the P5.js library and techniques from computational geometry, I was able to create an algorithm that generates a new image of a unique landscape each time it runs.


The results.


I was pleasantly surprised by the variety of images and 5-color palettes this program could generate. Although I never had an explicit purpose in mind when developing it, a few people reached out to me on Reddit to let me know that they were able to get some use from the palettes. Someone even suggested that I turn this into a wallpaper generator.

I don't think I will add much more to this project, but I've released the code under an MIT License on GitHub, so hopefully someone takes it and makes something fun with it.

Sketching, Ideating, and Simulating

Minimal, but impactful.

My main organizational goal was to keep the scope of this project small with output that would keep the user entertained for a while. Sometimes, even the simplest projects have potential to make an impact.

With simplicity in mind, while sketching and ideating, I determined the following features to be essential:

  • A mountain range generated by 1D Perlin Noise (a function included in P5.js)
  • Starry sky backgrounds
  • A single moon at various phases of the moon cycle
  • A 5-color or 3-color palette extracted from the image
  • Full mobile responsiveness
  • The ability to export the image to SVG or PNG format



Imitating natural systems -- or, the devil is in the noise details.

By applying a Perlin noise function with varying levels of noise detail to vector coordinates across the x and y axes, I was able to achieve output resembling a mountain range fading into the distance.

P5.js's built-in noise() function is powerful, but requires significant tweaking to achieve a desired look. I determined the "sweet spot" to base the mountains off to exist somewhere between images 4 and 7 (or 6 and 8 for mobile users).

Final Thoughts

Do you reap what you sow?

I realized while creating this program that all the hours I spent trying to create mountains with the Pen tool in Adobe Illustrator could have been trimmed down had I known about something like this. It really made me think: Is a single procedurally-generated organic shape inherently less "valuable" than similar shapes drawn by hand in Illustrator (or something similar)?

What is the value of a program that can generate an infinite number of graphics compared to an actual graphic designer?

I don't know.
