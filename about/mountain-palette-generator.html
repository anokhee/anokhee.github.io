Mountain Palettes Generator



Overview

About this project.

This is a small generative graphics program developed mostly with p5.js, plus some HTML/CSS interfacing programmed with the JavaScript DOM. It draws a unique landscape with an accompanying 5-color palette upon each page load. Feel free to play around with the demo or continue reading to learn more about the development process.



Inspiration & Background

Appalachian romanticism.

In parts of Georgia bordering North Carolina, the Blue Ridge Mountains hug the horizon from almost every conceivable angle. In sparsely-populated small towns dotted by pastures, warehouses, and gas stations, the snug adjacency to these mountains can evoke a certain emotional affect. Whether that leads to daydreams of an agrarian life in pre-colonial America or scenes from Deliverance varies, but is interesting nonetheless.


As someone prone to romanticism, I really wanted to artistically express my perception of the shapeshifting nature of mountains outside of photography. As a programmer, I figured that an interesting way to go about it would be with code.

With p5 and techniques from computational geometry, I was able to create an algorithm capable of generating an infinite number of unique landscapes each time it runs. The functionality is purposely minimal â€” I wanted to focus my efforts on the variety and visual appeal of the output versus focusing on the flashiness of the application itself.

Sketching, Ideating, and Simulating

Minimal, but impactful.

During planning, I aimed to keep the scope of this project as small as possible, focusing on output varied enough to keep the user entertained for a little while.While sketching and ideating, I determined the following features to be essential:

  • A mountain range generated by 1D Perlin Noise (a function included in P5.js)
  • Starry sky backgrounds
  • A single moon at various phases of the moon cycle
  • A 5-color or 3-color palette extracted from the image
  • Full mobile responsiveness
  • The ability to export the image to SVG or PNG format



Imitating natural systems -- or, the devil is in the noise details.

By applying a Perlin noise function with varying levels of noise detail to vector coordinates across the x and y axes, I was able to achieve output resembling a mountain range fading into the distance.

P5.js's built-in noise() function is powerful, but requires significant tweaking to achieve a desired look. I determined the "sweet spot" to base the mountains off to exist somewhere between images 4 and 7 (or 6 and 8 for mobile users).

Final Thoughts

Do you reap what you sow?

I realized that the hours I spent trying to create vector landscapes with Adobe Illustrator could have been trimmed down had I known about Perlin Noise. It really made me think: Is a single procedurally-generated organic shape inherently less "valuable" than those drawn by hand?

What is the value of a program that can generate an infinite number of graphics compared to an actual graphic designer? How special is uniqueness, really, when it's produced algorithmically?

I don't know.
