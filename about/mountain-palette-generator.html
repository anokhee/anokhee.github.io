Mountain Palette Generator

A single stretch of a mountain range can feel worlds away from another nearby.

I wanted to replicate the ethereality of mountainous skyscapes. Each image is algorithmically-generated, outputting unique mountains, skies, and color palettes each time the program runs.

Background

Imitating natural systems.

By applying a Perlin noise function with varying levels of noise detail to the y-position of vector points across the x-axis I was able to achieve output resembling a mountain range fading into the distance.

P5.js's built-in noise() function is powerful, but requires significant tweaking to achieve a desired look. I determined the "sweet spot" to base the mountains off to exist somewhere between images 4 and 7.

Process

Minimal, but impactful.

The idea was to keep the scope of this project small. During the sketching and ideating phase, I determined the following features essential enough to include in the project without overshadowing its original scope:

  • A mountain range generated by 1D Perlin Noise (included in P5.js)
  • Starry sky backgrounds
  • A single moon at various phases of the moon cycle
  • A 5-color or 3-color palette extracted from the image
  • Full mobile responsiveness
  • The ability to export the image to SVG or PNG format

Final Thoughts

The devil is in the (noise) details.

At one point, I considered scrapping this project altogether. No matter how much I tweaked the Perlin noise values, the output looked kind of unnatural to me. So, I experimented with other solutions. I tried adding small hills to the foreground, fractal trees, and even a flat horizon, but I found that the ideas that weren't ugly generally outgrew the scope.

Eventually, I figured out that a subtle yellowish gradient in between each mountain ridge added surprising depth to the images, while maintaining the simplicity defined in the project's original scope.


Reaping more than you sow?

As someone who loves painting and drawing, generative art poses a kind of philosophical question.

A single program that took under 3 days to write can produce infinitely more (unique) output than a graphic designer can create by hand over the course of several days.

Does generative art devalue handmade art or vice-versa? Are the two really comparable? IDK!
