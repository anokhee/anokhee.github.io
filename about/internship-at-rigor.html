Background

About Rigor

Rigor is a small SaaS company located in Atlanta, Georgia providing users with powerful tools to optimize and monitor their website's performance. With Rigor, users can access metrics related to user experience, APIs, and uptime, benchmark their site's performance against industry leaders, and diagnose and repair technical issues or answer business questions.


A creative and open-ended solo project

During the concluding week of my internship, I was given the opportunity to pursue my design interests via a 2-week solo project. At the end of the two weeks, I would present the idea to the engineering team. My only instruction was to find an area of the Rigor app that could be added to or improved upon, then design and develop a functional prototype of a solution.

The goal was not to deliver a production-ready new feature, but to spark discussion about user needs while accounting for how modern web design paradigms can be applied to the existing app.

Analytics Redesign

Because of my own concerns about it and a few mentions in Rigor’s technical support Slack channel, I decided to go with a redesign of this analytics chart.

The chart appears on the Results page of one of Rigor’s most popular features and displays information about various performance metrics based on the user’s custom configuration, then compares each metric with its results from the previous run.

Identifying pain points.

My first step was to identify the major pain points of the chart I was redesigning. Due to the 2-week I wasn't able to conduct user interviews. Fortunately, I was able to rely on my supervisor, coworkers, information gleaned from product tickets in JIRA, and my own experiences for insight about possible user frustrations.

Understanding that the prototype would be programmed into the existing React application, I figured that the most intuitive way to plan this project was to break the current chart down into its most basic components while proposing guiding questions along the way.

By doing this, I was able to construct the building blocks required to organize (and iteratively re-organize) the flow of information. I came up with a handful of painpoints that would serve as milestones while designing and developing this project:

  • The appearance of horizontal scrollbar when too many metrics are selected by the user
  • Poor information hierarchy making it difficult for users to derive meaningful information from the page at a glance (and no way to prioritize metrics)
  • Unnecessary UI components, like tooltips, flourishes, and links that clutter the page and distract from the "big picture"
  • Crowded layout with small text that may be inaccessible to users with poor vision

Understanding the users.

With information gathered throughout the semester from cross-departmental meetings, customer support tickets, and discussions with my supervisor, I synthesized a few personas to reflect Rigor's primary user base. These personas are based around age, level of experience with technology, role, and personality, with a few hypothetical questions to guide the design process.

Some generalizations I was able to make:

  • Rigor's users are, in general, more technically-savvy than the average Internet user
  • Ideally, users don't want to have to spend a lot of time in the Rigor app unless they are investigating an issue or configuring settings
  • Configurations should require as little modification as possible
  • The redesign is based on a desktop-only feature, so mobile-friendliness is not a factor to consider (yet)

Charts that answer questions.

One of my favorite parts of this project was trying to figure out how charts can be used to answer questions rather than exist for the sake of interactivity and visualization only. Because bar charts are widely known as some of the most intuitive charts to use, I came up with a few business questions to chart and represent the data in both the mockup and final presentation.

With more time, it would have been worthwhile to spend more time researching how visualizations can be used to answer questions, rather than exist for their own sake.


Protoype Implementation

D3 and React manipulate the DOM differently, which meant I had to brute-force some (arguably) icky code to build the interactivity and styling of the charts. With more time, I would have researched alternative libraries or methods of integrating the two libraries. From my understanding, this is a common problem with many solutions.

Challenge: Normalizing Data

At the beginning, I had some difficulty visualizing the data in a way that was immediately meaningful. The data is all displayed in wildly incompatible units like bytes, seconds, and counts, so what might be the ideal file count would almost certainly be an impossible byte size, and would not be helpful if displayed on the same graph.

After speaking with my supervisor, he suggested that the data might be more meaningful to users if it was converted into percentiles based on Amazon Web Services' Alexa Top Sites data.

To break down the percentile ranks from the 0th - 99th, we configured AWS to generate a JSON file with discrete data.

Now that I had the data in percentile ranks, I quickly encountered another issue: I would have to convert up to 3,860 unique values into individual percentiles. To accomplish this, I wrote an algorithm that normalized those values and mapped them to a D3 chart.
