No Platform Parity

Most frontline candidates engaged with Gracie through SMS and WhatsApp, so features needed to be delivered consistently across both desktop and mobile web.

Although these appear to be fairly simple design asks, the reality was that there were several factors I had to contend with.

1. Retrofitting Designs to Code

When I joined, the development team had been working without a designer for some time. They already built much of the application logic on the back end, but the user experience had yet to be defined.

Instead of the typical design-first process, I had to design patterns for the front end around the existing system architecture.

While keeping the codebase in mind constrained certain design possibilities, it also enabled me to move quickly and avoid engineering re-work by getting creative within those boundaries.

2. Limited Access to Irate Consumer Users

While recruiters loved Gracie for the convenience she brought to their daily tasks, candidates didn't love applying for jobs through an AI Chatbot.

Because Grayscale sold to recruiters, not jobseekers, I had limited access to user insights to design candidate-facing products around. However, I knew if the experience felt untrustworthy, candidates might drop-off mid-conversation, which was exactly what customers didn't want.

I read through jobhunting forums to understand how people applying to high-volume roles felt about chatbot-driven applications. The sentiment ranged from confusion to outright hostility, with the core fear being that a biased AI would filter them out before a human ever looked at their application.

3. The Business Case for Accessibility

The truth was that Grayscale handled candidate privacy seriously. I wanted to make that commitment visible to jobseekers by advocating internally for edge cases where applicants risked exclusion and designing UI patterns that reassured jobseekers that their application would be considered fairly.

However, as the sole designer, I needed to make a good case for anything that adds time to development work.

The business case for accessible, secure chatbot designs is that in high-volume hiring, customers need to keep their pipelines full. Making applications clear and accessible to as many candidates as possible directly serves that interest.

Solution

I designed conversational flows, mobile-first UI patterns, and plain-language microcopy that reduced friction while reinforcing trust. These interventions covered everything from document upload to demographic disclosure. A good example of this work is the redesigned EEOC survey experience, which worked consistently across SMS, WhatsApp, and web.

Impact

My design allowed candidates to complete EEOC surveys securely across all channels, while preserving conversational flow and strengthening trust in Gracie. This work helped Grayscale to maintain compliance at a massive scale, and contributed to improved completion rates.
