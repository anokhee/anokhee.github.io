About Tourial

Tourial is a B2B platform enabling users to build and publish interactive product tours. I was the first in-house design hire, working closely with PMs, Engineers, and the founders to designing early features and concepts. This work included:

  • An analytics dashboard for users to track how users were engaging with their product tours
  • A chrome extension enabling users to upload browser screenshots straight into Tourial
  • An improved form builder
  • A scalable integrations page

Key Contributions

  • Conducted discovery interviews with users to refine personas & drive new feature iterations
  • Validated prototypes and product requirements by conducting usability tests
  • Collaborated with Product and Engineering to develop designer-developer handoff processes
  • Worked on initial concept designs & visual rebranding

Sketching & Ideating

Tools Used: iPad, Pen + Paper

When I joined, Tourial wasn't even a year old yet, so much of my design work was open-ended and conceptual. The product direction at this stage was broad and required a lot of discovery to hone in on. I had opportunity to experiment with tons of different ideas and directions which came to me at random times. Sketching and doodling were a quick and easy way to put down stream-of-conscious ideas which popped up in the middle of a meeting, for example.



Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Tools Used: Figma, Balsamiq

For ideas that stuck, I refined the sketches by turning them into low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. I often included some interaction flows at this stage. For Product Managers, the interactions either visualized or informed the user stories. For Engineers, the wireframes visualized interactions, leading to tangible conversations around feasibility. For Business Development, it helped to see how things were going.

Insights Dashboard

Users didn't know how to optimize their product tours or understand their effectiveness.

Users could create and publish product tours to their websites, but they had no way of tracking engagement. They wanted to understand which parts of their tours were or weren't working, so they could optimize them.


Discovery with User Interviews

We sent out emails requesting 30-minute interviews from users. Fortunately for us, our users were happy to oblige. We used these interviews to find out more about each person's role at their company. We wanted to know how they used Tourial and how many others built/updated their product tours. We encouraged them to talk about each person's role and process. What other tools do they use on a daily basis? Based on this, we had an idea of what UI patterns they were comfortable with and used to.



First Iteration

After our initial interviews, we learned that our users wanted to see granular information about time spent per page, number of CTA clicks, and form views and submissions. I mocked up a handful of charts based on existing company data to get an idea of how it would look on a chart.



  • The funnel chart was the least favorable option – and not just because it resembled a DivaCup on Day 4. While it was good for visualizing drop-off data, it took up a lot of room and looked strange for nonlinear tours.
  • Click data could be well-represented with pie charts, but there was still something missing.
  • Line charts and bar charts worked well when displaying data for linear tours. By replacing the x-axis variable, the same chart could represent a variety of useful data. But again, the issue of nonlinear tours came up.
  • We hadn't yet figured out a good way to represent form interaction data on a chart.


Second Iteration

The second iteration made two things clear. We had some misconceptions about what users wanted and users didn't seem to know, either. In early interviews, users emphasized that they wanted to see granular interaction data, down to how many times someone hovered a link. But when presented with mockups showing just that, they said "I don't think I need that much detail!"

But...Why!? When reviewing recordings, I realized the blunder may have been on my end. Somewhere along the line, one user mentioned granularity, and I took it to heart. From then on, I started asking leading questions like, "Is this something you'd be interested in seeing?" Of course they'd say yes! Needless to say, we scrapped one of these screens.




Charts for Different Types of Tours

Bar charts didn't accurately reflect drop-off for different types of tours. The graphic on the right shows what real customers' tour data looked like when plotted on the bar graph. We worried that for nonlinear tours, the drop-off wasn't so straightforward. Surprisingly enough, customers didn't seem to mind this in the interviews. To them, these charts made enough sense to draw their own conclusions when reviewing analytics.



Third Iteration

The third iteration came about while I was experimenting with design system solutions. All designers know how time-consuming it is to put cohesive designs together in Figma without a component library. It's also time-consuming to create one in the first place. Honestly, it was really stressful!

After heavy discussion with engineers, I ended up buying a license of the UntitledUI kit to make mockups in Figma as fast as possible. The reasons I opted for UntitledUI over other options:

  • Developers didn't want to use a library like Material or Bootstrap, and UntitledUI didn't have any dependencies
  • UntitledUI had tons of generic SaaS components out-of-the-box, which meant non-designers could use it too, if needed.
  • It had nice charts, and I didn't want to make those by hand.
