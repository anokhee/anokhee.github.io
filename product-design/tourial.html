Tourial provides users with the ability to create and share interactive product tours to their marketing sites. I worked with product managers, engineers, and the company founders to deliver an insights dashboard, improved form builder, chrome extension and integrations page.

As the first in-house design hire, I had opportunity to significantly impact the product direction and processes, including setting up a design system in Figma and determining future branding.


Case Study: Insights Dashboard

Users didn't understand how their product tours were performing, or what steps they could take to optimize their effectiveness.

01.

The Problem

What We Heard from Users

While Tourial's existing insights dashboard enabled users to track the number of visitors who clicked through their product tour, they found it challenging to derive actionable insights from this data. They wanted to know where their visitors were getting lost or losing interest in their product tour, so that they could optimize it accordingly.



02.

Discovery

Interviewing Power Users

We reached out to our power users and requested interviews to gain a better understanding of their needs and expectations. During these interviews, we asked questions to learn more about each user's role at their company, how they used Tourial, and who was responsible for creating and updating their product tours.



03.

Prototyping

First Iteration

Based on what we learned from our discovery sessions, I created dashboard mockups displaying visualizations of the metrics users considered most important, including views per page, CTA clicks, and form data.

I explored a variety of different chart options, including funnel, pie, line, and bar charts, to display information about user engagement at varying levels of granularity. Users responded well to a few of these designs, and their feedback provided us with a good idea of which concepts to scrap or expand upon for the next round.



03.

Prototyping

Nonlinear Product Tours

During our discovery sessions, we realized that we didn't considers how data might display for nonlinear tours – or tours that did not follow a chronological order from start to finish – because it was unclear where the drop-off point occurred.

Interestingly, we learned that this issue was more of a concern internally than for our end-users, who were comfortable drawing their own conclusions from the available data. Although it was confusing, this feedback helped us prioritize which features to focus on.



03.

Prototyping

Second Iteration

The second iteration revealed some misconceptions about what users wanted. In early interviews, users emphasized that they wanted more granular interaction data, down to how many times someone hovered a link. But when presented with mockups showing a greater level of detail, they didn't seem to be as interested in granular interaction data as much as they were interested in how this data varied at different points during the product tour.



04.

Handoff

Designer-Developer Collaboration

Based on feedback from our discover sessions with users, I created dashboard mockups displaying visualizations of views per page, CTA clicks, and form data. In this iteration, I explored different chart options, including funnel, pie, line, and bar charts, to display granular information about user engagement. Although users responded well to a few of these designs and their feedback provided us with a good starting point, we realized that we didn't account for nonlinear tours or form data.
