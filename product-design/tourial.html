Overview

Tourial provides users with the ability to create and share interactive product tours to their marketing sites. I worked with product managers, engineers, and the company founders to deliver an insights dashboard, improved form builder, chrome extension and integrations page.

As the first in-house design hire, I had opportunity to significantly impact the product direction and processes, including setting up a design system in Figma and determining future branding.

Insights Dashboard

Problem

Users didn't understand how their product tours were performing, or what steps they could take to optimize their effectiveness. Users wanted to know where visitors were getting lost or losing interest in their product tours, so that they could improve lead conversion.

While Tourial's existing insights dashboard enabled users to track the number of visitors who clicked through their product tour, they found it challenging to derive actionable insights from the limited amount of data provided.


Discovery

In order to better understand what our users expected out of Tourial's built-in analytics, we requested discovery interviews from our power users and performed competitive analysis.

The interviews were rather open-ended and exploratory. We asked questions to learn more about each user's role at their company, how they used Tourial, and who was responsible for creating and updating their product tours. While we focused our discovery around the Insights Dashboard, we kept an open mind around applying this feedback to other areas of the app.


Prototyping

The prototyping phase consisted of several build-measure-learn loops. We presented prototypes, iterated on them, and then presented them again to the same set of users. User feedback uncovered opportunities for improvement and helped us decide which concepts to discard or explore further.


First Iteration

Based on our initial discovery sessions, I created several dashboard mockups displaying the metrics users considered most important, with a focus on displaying user engagement data at varying levels of granularity.

The feedback we received helped us narrow our scope and understand which representations of data resonated with users. Funnel charts were unpopular, for example, because they emphasized conversion numbers without providing meaningful insights into the underlying reasons.


Nonlinear Product Tours

During our discovery sessions, we realized that we didn't consider how data might display for nonlinear tours.

Interestingly, we learned that this issue was more of a concern internally than for our end-users, who turned out to feel comfortable drawing their own conclusions when this data was displayed on a mockup of a bar chart. Although displaying data for nonlinear tours was still important, we were able to prioritize it accordingly.

Second Iteration

In early interviews, users expressed a desire to see more granular interaction data, down to how many times a visitor hovered a link. But when presented with mockups providing that level of detail, users struggled to envision how they could use that data to convert a visitor into a lead.

We realized that we may have assumed too much. Instead of focusing on our two user problems, – "Users don't understand how their product tours are performing" and "Users don't know what steps they can take to optimize their effectiveness" – we should have been asking "How might we help users convert more visitors into leads?"
