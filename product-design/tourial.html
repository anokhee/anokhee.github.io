About Tourial

Tourial is a platform designed for business-to-business (B2B) use, providing users with the ability to create and share interactive product tours. As the initial in-house design hire, I collaborated closely with product managers, engineers, and the company founders to develop and design early features and concepts for the platform. This work encompassed the following tasks:

  • An analytics dashboard for users to track how users were engaging with their product tours
  • A chrome extension enabling users to upload browser screenshots straight into Tourial
  • An improved form builder
  • A scalable integrations page

Key Contributions

  • Conducted discovery interviews with users to refine personas & drive new feature iterations
  • Validated prototypes and product requirements by conducting usability tests
  • Collaborated with Product and Engineering to develop designer-developer handoff processes
  • Worked on initial concept designs & visual rebranding

Sketching & Ideating

Tools Used: iPad, Pen + Paper

When I started working at Tourial, the company was less than a year old, and there was a lot of room for exploration and conceptual work in my design role. At this stage, the product direction was broad, and a lot of research was necessary to narrow it down. I had the chance to experiment with many different ideas and directions that came to me at random times. I found that sketching and doodling were an effective way to capture stream-of-conscious ideas that popped up during meetings or other times.



Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Tools Used: Figma, Balsamiq

To refine my ideas, I converted the sketches that had potential into low-fidelity wireframes using Figma. At this stage, I often included some interaction flows. These interactions served as visualizations or informed user stories for Product Managers, visualized interactions for Engineers, and helped Business Development see progress.



High-Fidelity Mockups & Prototypes

Tools Used: Figma

High fidelity mockups allow for realistic user testing, identifying design issues and gathering feedback to refine the design. They also build stakeholder confidence by providing a clear representation of the final product.



Developer Handoff

Tools Used: Figma

Using Figma made developer handoff generally easy, however, with remote work and multiple features being worked on simultaneously, it became difficult to track everything. To simplify the handoff process, I added design notes that provided additional details on interactions, reducing the need for back-and-forth communication.

Insights Dashboard

Users didn't know how to optimize their product tours or understand their effectiveness.

Tourial's Insights Dashboard provides a way for users to track the performance of their product tours and optimize them for better engagement. By analyzing user behavior data, such as click-through rates and time spent on each step of the tour, users can understand which parts of their tours are effective and which need improvement. With this information, they can make data-driven decisions to optimize their tours for better engagement.


Discovery with User Interviews

We sent out emails requesting 30-minute interviews from users. Fortunately for us, our users were happy to oblige. We used these interviews to find out more about each person's role at their company. We wanted to know how they used Tourial and how many others built/updated their product tours. We encouraged them to talk about each person's role and process. What other tools do they use on a daily basis? Based on this, we had an idea of what UI patterns they were comfortable with, and how we could leverage knowledge of the products they were currently using to fulfill their needs with Tourial.



First Iteration

Users wanted detailed analytics about engagement with their product tours, so we conducted initial interviews to determine what metrics were important to them. Based on their feedback, we created mockups of charts to display data such as time spent per page, CTA clicks, and form views and submissions.



The team explored different chart options, including funnel, pie, line, and bar charts, to display granular information about user engagement. However, none of these options worked well for nonlinear tours or form interaction data.



Second Iteration

The second iteration made two things clear. We had some misconceptions about what users wanted and users didn't seem to know, either. In early interviews, users emphasized that they wanted to see granular interaction data, down to how many times someone hovered a link. But when presented with mockups showing just that, they said "I don't think I need that much detail!"

But...Why!? When reviewing recordings, I realized the blunder may have been on my end. Somewhere along the line, one user mentioned granularity, and I took it to heart. From then on, I started asking leading questions like, "Is this something you'd be interested in seeing?" Of course they'd say yes! Needless to say, we scrapped one of these screens.




Charts for Different Types of Tours

Bar charts didn't accurately reflect drop-off for different types of tours. The graphic on the right shows what real customers' tour data looked like when plotted on the bar graph. We worried that for nonlinear tours, the drop-off wasn't so straightforward. Surprisingly enough, customers didn't seem to mind this in the interviews. To them, these charts made enough sense to draw their own conclusions when reviewing analytics.



Third Iteration

The third iteration came about while I was experimenting with design system solutions. All designers know how time-consuming it is to put cohesive designs together in Figma without a component library. It's also time-consuming to create one in the first place. Honestly, it was really stressful!

After heavy discussion with engineers, I ended up buying a license of the UntitledUI kit to make mockups in Figma as fast as possible. The reasons I opted for UntitledUI over other options:

  • Developers didn't want to use a library like Material or Bootstrap, and UntitledUI didn't have any dependencies
  • UntitledUI had tons of generic SaaS components out-of-the-box, which meant non-designers could use it too, if needed.
  • It had nice charts, and I didn't want to make those by hand.
