About RoadSync

RoadSync is a logistics and payments software used by a variety of users, including both B2B and B2C. Its B2B users include warehouses, carrier services, and tow and repair services, while its B2C users are the truck drivers making deliveries. During my time at RoadSync, the company's goal was to expand its B2C user base and establish itself as the go-to application for these drivers on the road. As the first in-house designer, I had the opportunity to make early product and visual design decisions. I also ideated and created the first round of designs for the mobile app.

Low Driver Signup Rates

RoadSync's Driver App targeted B2C customers, mainly long-haul truck drivers. However, despite providing signup instructions, we only saw about 10 signups each week. To address this, I redesigned the signup flow with user-friendly design, informative text, and illustrations.

Before Redesign

HMW motivate drivers to sign up for a RoadSync Driver account?

  • Emphasize ease of account creation.
  • Communicate immediate value to drivers by describing how it can solve their frustrations.
  • Build credibility through usable design.

HMW make it easier for them to sign up?

  • Use recognizable UX patterns.
  • Keep the form as short as possible at the beginning, giving them the option to add more information later.





Mobile Concepts

Spearheading RoadSync's Native App

All of RoadSync's products were offered either for desktop-only or the responsive web, but we saw opportunity for its B2C driver app to be more useful to users as a native mobile app. I was responsible for the initial concept designs that we pitched to stakeholders for the mobile app.


Why we pivoted to mobile:

  • Offline capability would allow truck drivers to create invoices in remote areas without internet access and submit them later when they have an internet connection.
  • Push notifications can provide a secure way to update users as they can deliver notifications to specific devices without disclosing their personal data.
  • A mobile app can centralize schedules, invoices, and payments, providing users with a convenient and efficient hub for managing their business on-the-go.






My Design Process

What did RoadSync's users need?

We expanded our product offerings to truck drivers, leveraging the potential B2C users generated by our B2B users. However, drivers were hesitant to share information about themselves, making it difficult to understand their needs. Our challenge was to uncover insights about people who were reluctant to be known.




Making Sense of Insights from User Research

After conducting preliminary research, we had more information than we knew what to do with. Affinity Maps and Empathy Maps came in handy for us to refer to as we drafted a few User Personas. From there, we could jump into Journey Mapping with real, human users in mind.





Low-Fidelity to Collaborate and Validate

I started designs as sketches, which turned into low-fidelity mockups, allowing me to freely experiment with various user flows and interactions. By putting low-fidelity, clickable prototypes in front of users, we could collect feedback about whether the design fulfilled the user needs before spending time on making the designs pixel-perfect.
